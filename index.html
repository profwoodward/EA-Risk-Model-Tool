Companion to: Quantifying Compromise Risk in Exceptional Access Architectures Under Deep Uncertainty

The Risk-Side
Question Set

A structured elicitation tool. Twelve questions over architectural scope, adversary environment, operational controls, deployment, and decision framework — each mapped to a parameter in the underlying model.

The output is a range under the assumptions you specify. Structural findings (architectural ordering, tail-shape direction) do not change with your answers; calibration-conditional magnitudes do. Read the outputs accordingly.

Tolerability context — safety-regulator benchmarks

Every field that quantifies catastrophic risk — life-safety1, nuclear, aviation, financial tail risk — shares one principle: a single event that harms many people must be made rarer the more people it harms. The shaded bands apply that principle at the scale you set, calibrated to the only published anchor for it (HSE's societal F‑N criterion: slope −1 through 50 deaths at 1-in-5,000/year)2. We make no claim that a compromise is equivalent to a death3: the bands treat one person's compromise as no worse than one fatality purely as an upper bound, and the figure shows how far beneath that you can discount before the verdict changes. Switch the consequence basis below to economic loss for the same comparison with no fatalities in the frame.

The criterion is denominated in fatalities; here N is read as people seriously affected, and values beyond N=1,000 extrapolate past HSE's truncation2. The aviation marker (catastrophic ≤ 10−9) is per flight-hour, a different exposure base4. The bands describe society's tolerance for an N‑fatality event; the comparison does not assert that a compromise is a death. And the harm is not merely “privacy”: a stolen access capability is a single point of mass compromise with economic, national-security and — for journalists, dissidents and abuse victims — physical-safety consequences, so a one-dimensional reading understates it.6

Has any system ever achieved these levels? No deployed system handling comparable key material has demonstrated a compromise rate near even the intolerable boundary. The most safety-critical engineering aims at 10−7–10−9/yr, but those figures are established by analysis, not observation: they sit below what testing can confirm, and where the record can be checked — civil nuclear — the observed core-damage rate (~3 events in ~14,000 reactor-years, ≈ 8×10−4/yr) runs about an order of magnitude above its own design target5. Observed compromise rates for high-value key infrastructure are ~10−2/yr — the model's own empirical input7 — so the tolerability threshold has never been approached, let alone met.

What “compromise” means here. The modelled consequence is not a partial breach of a few fields but total exposure of a user’s communications — the capability a stolen access mechanism actually confers. The nearest real-world analogues are total-device spyware such as Pegasus and full-archive leaks such as the 2015 Ashley Madison breach, whose documented harms run from indefinite extortion and ruin to, for those most at risk, detention, violence and death.9 Read against that, the loss figures above are a floor, not a ceiling.

Notes & sources

1 HSE individual-risk tolerability. HSE, Reducing Risks, Protecting People (R2P2, 2001), developing the 1992 Tolerability of Risk from Nuclear Power Stations: three regions — unacceptable, tolerable only if ALARP, broadly acceptable. Risk of death per year is judged intolerable above 1×10−3 for workers and 1×10−4 for the public, and broadly acceptable below 1×10−6. (HSE SPC/Permissioning/37, “Guidance on ALARP Decisions in COMAH”.)

2 HSE societal F‑N criterion — and the limit of this chart's use of it. R2P2 (§136) gives a single societal anchor: an event killing 50 or more people is intolerable if its frequency exceeds 1-in-5,000 per year, placed on an F‑N line of slope −1 and truncated at N=1,000. This chart applies that line at far larger N. HSE does not endorse the criterion beyond its N=1,000 truncation, so the bands at population scale are an illustrative extrapolation, not an HSE-sanctioned threshold; treat them as order-of-magnitude.

3 No equivalence between a compromise and a death. The bands treat one person's compromise as no worse than one fatality purely as an upper bound; no moral equivalence is asserted. The break-even shown is g* = (intolerable frequency) ÷ (model median): the per-person gravity at which the median just reaches the intolerable line. The conclusion holds for any valuation graver than g*, so it does not depend on the exchange rate between privacy loss and life. The harm of a mass compromise is in any case multidimensional — economic, national-security and, for at-risk groups, physical — so a purely “privacy” reading understates it.

4 Civil aviation. CS‑25.1309 / FAA AC 25.1309‑1B: catastrophic failure conditions must be “extremely improbable”, ≤ ~1×10−9 per flight-hour, apportioned from a ~1×10−7 per-flight-hour aircraft-level target across ~100 conditions. This is a per-flight-hour basis, not an annual rate, and the 10−9 figure is established by analysis and redundancy, not by observation. Shown only as a reference marker.

5 Civil nuclear: target vs record. Design core-damage-frequency targets are ~10−4–10−5 per reactor-year. The empirical record — roughly three severe core-damage events (Three Mile Island, Chernobyl, Fukushima) in ~14,000 reactor-years, ≈ 7.6×10−4 per reactor-year — runs about an order of magnitude above target. (NRDC 2011 reactor-year tally; HSE/EC core-damage-frequency reviews.)

6 Exposure bases are not strictly commensurable. The model's τ is an annual rate; the National Risk Register measures over 2 years (malicious) or 5 years (non-malicious); aviation uses per-flight-hour. Cross-domain comparisons here are order-of-magnitude, not like-for-like, and the “people affected” N is itself an assumption set by the reader.

7 Model outputs. The median, interval and tail are the paper's calibration-conditional values; this tool is a structured interpolation of that model, not a re-derivation. The ~10−2/yr empirical compromise rate cited is the paper's own evidence pillar.

8 Economic basis — value of preventing a fatality. The economic toggle converts the loss given compromise (L) into fatality-equivalents, L ÷ VPF, and applies the same societal F‑N criterion. VPF is HM Treasury's value of preventing a fatality, ~£2m (about £1.96m in 2018 prices, indexed to GDP per head; HM Treasury Green Book and DfT TAG Data Book), used across the DfT, HSE and Office for Nuclear Regulation. This is the standard regulatory cost-benefit bridge, so it removes fatalities from the picture only in presentation — the calibration still derives from the fatality criterion. VPF is a willingness-to-pay construct and is itself contested (e.g. Thomas & Vaughan, 2015, argue its evidential base is weak). The default loss (£12bn) is illustrative; set it to the paper's Lcomp as preferred.

9 Total exposure — real-world analogues. A compromised access mechanism confers total access to a user's communications, comparable to NSO Group's Pegasus spyware, which grants an operator a device's messages, photos, contacts, camera, microphone and location. Human-rights investigators have documented people being detained, tortured and, in some cases, killed after such targeting — including associates of journalist Jamal Khashoggi, whose phones were targeted around his 2018 killing. The public-dump analogue is the 2015 Ashley Madison breach (~32 million users; names, private messages and intimate data exposed), which fuelled years of extortion and sextortion and was linked to at least two suicides. These illustrate that the harm of total exposure is open-ended and only partly monetisable, so per-record breach costs understate it.
